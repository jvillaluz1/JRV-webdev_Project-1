My Project Wingman Review (or Analysis)

What is Project Wingman?

To put it simply, Project Wingman is an indie arcade flight game. I would describe it as a love letter to the Ace Combat franchise, as it shares similar gameplay elements.

The game features two campaigns: the base campaign and a DLC called Frontline-59, which was exclusive to PlayStation 5. Additionally, there is a mode called Conquest, a wave-based survival mode where missions progressively increase in difficulty.

The Base Campaign

For the sake of time, I will not go into deep detail about the plot, as both the base campaign and Frontline-59 have complex narratives.

In the base campaign, you play as a mercenary pilot in the Sicario Mercenary Corps, specifically as part of the Hitman Squadron. Your callsign? Monarch.

The story takes place centuries after a catastrophic tectonic event known as "The Calamity," which triggered massive destruction across the Pacific Ring of Fire. Fast forward about 400 years, new global powers have emerged, and the world has changed drastically.

Among these powers is the Pacific Federation, a coalition of former governments, corporations, and military institutions that control vast territories and resources. At the beginning of the game, you and your squadron finish a contract in the Caribbean, destroying a Federation cargo containing a volatile element called Cordium, an energy-rich geothermal resource over which the Federation holds a monopoly.

After this mission, you and the Sicario Mercenary Corps get involved in the Cascadian Civil War for profit. According to Stardust, a Cascadian National Guard pilot, Cascadia was once an associate of the Federation but sought independence over fears of "federalization" and the relentless exploitation of its Cordium reserves to fuel the Federation’s war machine.

As the conflict escalates, you begin to witness the war's true devastation, including the complete destruction of the Cascadian city of Prospero by Cordium cruise missiles. This event sets off a second catastrophe, causing the situation to spiral out of control.

Frontline-59

At some point during the Cascadian Civil War, the Cascadian forces and their mercenary allies gain the upper hand after a decisive victory over the Pacific Federation at the Bering Strait. The Federation's forces are nearly wiped out—by the time Frontline-59's first mission begins, only a dozen active Federation fighter squadrons remain.

With the Federation on the brink of collapse, Crystal Kingdom (Federation High Command) issues an emergency recall, deploying reserve squadrons to reinforce the war effort. However, their intervention comes too late. Many of the Federation’s elite Peacekeeper Squadrons are forced to retreat, leaving you, K-9A "Driver," and the rest of the K-9 Reserve Division to fight against overwhelming odds.

Fortunately, Driver’s exceptional piloting skills allow your squadron to secure a fighting chance, covering the retreat of the remaining Federation forces.

Unlike the base campaign, where you fight for Cascadia and the mercenaries, Frontline-59 puts you in the cockpit of a Federation pilot, tasked with defending key Cordium geothermal facilities from Cascadian and mercenary attacks. If these facilities fall, the Federation faces an inevitable collapse.

Conquest

Conquest mode is a more freeform experience, allowing you to lead a squadron in a series of progressively difficult missions.

There isn’t a defined story for this mode, leaving players to create their own headcanons or fan fiction based on the game's lore. The gameplay revolves around completing missions, earning points, and unlocking new aircraft and even airships to aid you in your conquests.

The Gameplay of Project Wingman

If you’re familiar with the Ace Combat series, you’ll feel right at home. The game features a wide array of weaponry, including standard STMD Fox-2s (infrared missiles), MLAAs (multi-lock anti-air missiles), UGBLs (unguided bombs), and MLAGs (multi-lock anti-ground missiles). With so many different missile types, you’ll find a playstyle that suits you.

Unlike hardcore flight simulators, your primary concerns aren’t fuel management or navigation— it’s the relentless barrage of enemy missiles and dogfights. Dodging these while taking out priority targets is the key to survival. Oh, and try not to crash into large planes or airships.

The game is visually stunning. Ignore the low-resolution ground textures, and you’ll see that every frame looks like a painting. Project Wingman even supports VR, which, while overwhelming, provides an incredibly immersive experience.
